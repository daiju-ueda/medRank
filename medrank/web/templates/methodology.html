How this works

Methodology

A ranking is only as trustworthy as the rule behind it. Here is every rule we use.

Where the data comes from

Every figure on MedRank is derived from OpenAlex, an open, freely licensed (CC0) catalog of the world's scholarly work. We take a monthly snapshot of its author records and recompute the entire site from scratch. We never edit the underlying numbers.

Who counts as a “medical researcher”

MedRank is a map of biomedical research — from the clinic to the lab bench — not just of clinicians. We include an author when the primary topic of their work falls in the Health Sciences domain (clinical medicine, public health, nursing, pharmacology, dentistry and related fields), or in one of three Life Sciences fields that drive modern medicine: Biochemistry, Genetics & Molecular Biology, Immunology & Microbiology, and Neuroscience.

To keep the map to real researchers rather than one-off authors, we require at least 5 publications and an h-index of 2 or more. This threshold is deliberately low: raising it would erase early-career researchers and those outside the largest research economies, which is exactly the part of the map worth keeping.

How the rankings are computed

H-index, citations and publications are taken directly from the author's record and ranked, worldwide and within each field and country.

Rising stars measures momentum: citations received in the last three years against the three years before, with a gentle correction so that a large, established body of work is not mistaken for a sudden surge.

Most consistent rewards a steady hand — researchers whose yearly citations are both well-distributed across the decade and low in year-to-year swing, rather than a single spike.

Young guns restricts the ranking to researchers whose publishing career spans ten years or less, then ranks them by h-index.

Honest limits

Author name disambiguation is hard, and OpenAlex — like every such system — occasionally merges or splits people. Field boundaries are fuzzy: a neuroscientist studying cognition sits next to one studying stroke. A researcher with no listed institution is shown as Unaffiliated rather than dropped. We surface what the data says; we do not claim it is the last word on anyone's career.
